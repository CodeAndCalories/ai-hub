AI Phone Answering Service vs. Hiring a Receptionist: An Honest Comparison

No fluff — just a real breakdown of what each option costs, where each one falls short, and which makes sense depending on your situation.

March 25, 2025 · AI Hub

If you're researching how to stop missing calls, you've probably looked at a few options: hire someone to answer the phone, use a traditional answering service, or try one of the newer AI phone systems. Each has real advantages and real drawbacks. Here's the honest version.

Option 1: Hire a Full-Time Receptionist

What you get: A person who knows your business, answers calls in real time, builds rapport with callers, can handle complex situations, and is there for whatever comes up during the day.

What it costs: A full-time receptionist in most U.S. markets runs $35,000–$50,000 a year in salary, plus benefits, payroll taxes, PTO, and training. All-in, you're likely looking at $45,000–$65,000 annually for a role that covers roughly 8 hours a day, 5 days a week.

The gaps: After 5pm, on weekends, during lunch, when they're sick, when they quit — nobody's answering the phone. For a business where leads call at unpredictable hours, this is a real problem. You're also managing a person, which takes time and energy beyond the salary.

Best for: Businesses with high call volume during predictable hours, where the receptionist does more than just answer calls (scheduling, admin, client management), and where a person in the role is genuinely the right answer.

Option 2: Traditional Answering Service

What you get: A shared pool of human agents — usually at an offshore or domestic call center — who answer calls on behalf of multiple businesses. They follow a script you provide and take messages.

What it costs: Typically $50–$300/month depending on call volume and hours covered. Some offer 24/7 coverage, which bumps the price up.

The gaps: Quality is inconsistent. The agents don't know your business, they follow a script, and when callers go off-script the experience falls apart. Callers can usually tell they've reached a generic service, which isn't a great first impression. Summaries and messages vary in quality and speed.

Best for: Businesses that mostly need after-hours message-taking and don't get a lot of complex calls. Works fine as a safety net but not great as a primary call handler.

Option 3: AI Phone Answering Service

What you get: A system that answers calls instantly, any time of day, with a natural-sounding voice. It can carry on an actual back-and-forth conversation, ask follow-up questions, answer basic questions about your business, collect lead information, and route or summarize the call.

What it costs: Most AI phone services are in the $50–$300/month range, similar to traditional answering services, with some variation based on call minutes or features.

The gaps: It can't replace you for complex, judgment-heavy conversations — offers, negotiations, upset clients. It may stumble on highly unusual questions. And some systems still sound robotic enough that callers notice, which can be off-putting.

Best for: Small businesses and solo operators who need coverage at all hours, want consistent quality without managing a person, and get a mix of simple and moderate-complexity calls.

The Situation That Drives Most People to AI

Most small business owners don't go looking for an AI phone system because they thought it sounded interesting. They do it because they just lost a deal to a missed call, or they did the math on missed calls and felt sick, or they tried an answering service and got complaints about the call quality.

The typical profile is: solo operator or small team, call volume that doesn't justify a full-time receptionist, business hours that don't match when leads call, and a need for something that works without being managed closely.

For realtors especially, the AI option has become a default choice because the call timing problem is so severe. Buyers call during showings. Sellers call on Sunday morning. Nobody is waiting for business hours.

When a Human Receptionist Is Still the Right Call

There are situations where nothing beats a good human receptionist. If your business gets a high volume of calls that require real judgment — detailed consultations, sensitive client conversations, complex scheduling — a person in that role adds value that a system can't match.

If your clients specifically value the personal touch of reaching a person when they call, that matters. Some industries — medical, legal, high-end services — have callers where an AI receptionist would undermine the relationship rather than support it.

And if you can afford it and the call volume justifies it, a great receptionist who knows your business is a real asset.

The Practical Answer for Most Small Businesses

If you're a solo agent, a small team, or a service business owner who gets 10–80 calls a week and can't afford or justify a full-time receptionist — the AI option is probably the right starting point. The cost is manageable, the setup is fast, and the coverage problem gets solved immediately.

You can always add a human later if the business grows to the point where it makes sense. But most people who try AI phone coverage and set it up properly find that it handles a lot more than they expected.

The test worth doing: call your own business number right now and see what a new customer experiences. If what you heard isn't good enough for someone you want to close a deal with, it's worth fixing.
