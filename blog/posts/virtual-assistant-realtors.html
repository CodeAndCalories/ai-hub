Virtual Assistant for Realtors: The Phone Problem Nobody Talks About

A VA can handle your inbox and your calendar. But calls are harder — they happen in real time, any time, and can't be batched. Here's what actually works.

March 18, 2025 · AI Hub

The virtual assistant conversation in real estate usually goes like this: agent gets busy enough that they can't keep up with email, they hire a VA to handle admin tasks, and they feel noticeably less stressed within a month. Good VAs are worth every dollar.

But then the phone rings during a showing. And the VA isn't there for that. Because phone coverage is a completely different problem.

Why Phone and Admin Are Different Problems

Email and calendar management work well with a VA because they're asynchronous. Your VA can process your inbox at 9am and again at 2pm. Tasks come in, tasks go out. The work is predictable enough to batch.

Phone calls don't work that way. They happen on the caller's schedule, not yours. A buyer who saw your listing at 7pm and wants to schedule a showing right now is not going to wait until your VA's next block. Neither is a seller calling to check in before their open house. Neither is an agent from the other side of a deal who needs a quick answer.

Some VAs do offer phone coverage, but the economics get complicated fast. You're usually paying for their time whether the phone rings or not, and after-hours coverage often means a premium rate or a separate service entirely. For most solo agents or small teams, dedicated phone VA coverage is either too expensive or too inconsistent.

What Realtors Actually Need on the Phone

Think about the calls you get in a typical week. They roughly break into:

New buyer inquiries. Someone found your number through a listing, a sign, Zillow, Google, a referral. They want to know if the property is still available, what the price is, whether they can see it. They're warm leads who will call someone else if you don't answer.

Seller check-ins. Existing clients who want a status update. They're not in a rush, but they're anxious and want to feel heard. A voicemail feels dismissive to them.

Agent-to-agent calls. Showing confirmations, offer discussions, deal questions. These often need a real answer from you, not a message-taker.

Spam and solicitations. The calls you don't want to take anyway.

The first two categories are where missed calls hurt you the most. New buyers can't be recovered once they've moved on. Sellers who feel ignored start looking for another agent.

The Case for an AI Phone Assistant

For the phone side of things, an AI assistant has some structural advantages over a human VA:

It's available around the clock. Not just business hours, not just when coverage is scheduled. Every call, whenever it comes in, gets answered.

The cost doesn't scale with call volume. Whether you get 5 calls a day or 50, the price is the same. A human VA charging by the hour doesn't work that way.

It doesn't have bad days. A human who's tired or distracted gives a worse call experience. An AI is consistent.

It can be configured for your specific listings and business. You can give it information about your current properties, your availability, how you want leads prioritized, and what questions to ask callers.

What It Can't Do (Be Honest About This)

An AI phone assistant isn't a replacement for you on an important call. If a buyer is ready to make an offer, they need to talk to you. If a seller is upset about something, they need a real person. If an agent from the other side calls with a negotiation question, that's not a call you want fielded by software.

Good systems handle this by letting you define escalation rules. Calls from known contacts, or calls where the AI detects urgency, get routed to your cell. Everything else gets handled and summarized.

The goal isn't to remove yourself from important conversations — it's to make sure you never miss a new lead because you were busy with one of those conversations.

Putting It Together: VA + AI Phone Coverage

The setup that works for most busy agents is actually a combination. Your VA handles email, scheduling, transaction coordination, and any tasks that can be done asynchronously. Your AI phone system handles inbound calls, captures leads, and routes urgent ones to you.

They don't overlap much, and together they cover most of the administrative load that keeps agents from focusing on actually selling houses.

The VA builds a great admin foundation. The phone system makes sure none of the business flowing in through the phone gets lost while you're using that foundation.

Setting It Up Without Overcomplicating It

A lot of agents put this off because it feels like a technology project. It doesn't have to be. Most AI phone systems are designed to be configured in an afternoon, not a week. You set up your business number to forward to the service (or use a new number it provides), write a brief description of your business and what callers typically need, and test a few calls to make sure it sounds right.

The configuration questions you'll need to answer:

  • What should the AI say when it answers? (Usually something like "Thanks for calling [Your Name] Real Estate — how can I help you today?")
  • What are your current listings and basic details about each?
  • What questions should it ask new leads? (Name, what they're looking for, timeline, contact info)
  • When should it route calls to you directly vs. take a message?
  • How do you want summaries delivered — text, email, both?

That's most of it. Once it's running, you tweak based on the calls you see coming through. Most agents are happy with it within the first week.

The phone problem doesn't go away on its own. The market doesn't get slower, buyers don't get more patient, and your schedule doesn't get easier. But it's one of the more solvable problems in real estate once you actually sit down and solve it.
